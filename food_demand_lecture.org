* The Consumer's Problem
** Consumers Make Choices
Most of us do not solve a linear program at the grocery store.  Instead:
- We have a /budget/ --- how much we can spend.
- We face /prices/ --- what each food costs.
- We have /preferences/ --- what we like to eat.

The consumer's problem: choose the /best/ bundle of goods you can afford.

** An Everyday Example
Suppose you have $10 for lunch.  You can buy:
- Burritos at $5 each
- Salads at $4 each

What do you choose?  It depends on how much you /like/ each option relative to its price.

** Making This Precise
Economists write the consumer's problem as:
\[
  \max_{c} U(c) \quad \text{subject to} \quad p'c \leq x
\]

- \(c\) :: the /consumption bundle/ (quantities of each food)
- \(p\) :: the /price vector/ (price per unit of each food)
- \(x\) :: the /budget/ (total money available)
- \(U(c)\) :: /utility/ --- a number measuring how satisfied you are

** What Is Utility?
- A way to /rank/ bundles: if \(U(A) > U(B)\), you prefer bundle \(A\).
- Not a measure of "happiness units" --- only the ranking matters.
- We assume utility is:
  - /Increasing/ --- more food (weakly) preferred to less.
  - /Concave/ --- variety is good; diminishing returns to any single food.

* Indifference Curves and Budget Lines
** Indifference Curves
An /indifference curve/ connects all bundles that give the same utility.

- Every point on the curve is equally good.
- Curves further from the origin are better.
- Curves never cross (why?).

** The Budget Line
The budget line shows all bundles that exactly exhaust your budget:
\[
  p_1 c_1 + p_2 c_2 = x
\]

- Slope :: \(-p_1/p_2\) --- the rate at which the market lets you trade one good for another.
- Intercepts :: \(x/p_1\) on the \(c_1\) axis, \(x/p_2\) on the \(c_2\) axis.

** Finding the Best Choice
The consumer picks the bundle on the /highest/ indifference curve that still touches the budget line.

At the optimum, the indifference curve is /tangent/ to the budget line:
\[
  \frac{MU_1}{MU_2} = \frac{p_1}{p_2}
\]

- Left side :: your /marginal rate of substitution/ (MRS) --- how much \(c_2\) you'd give up for one more unit of \(c_1\).
- Right side :: the /price ratio/ --- how much \(c_2\) the market requires you to give up.

* A Concrete Example: Cobb-Douglas
** Cobb-Douglas Utility
A simple and widely-used functional form:
\[
  U(c_1, c_2) = \alpha_1 \log c_1 + \alpha_2 \log c_2
\]

- \(\alpha_1, \alpha_2 > 0\) measure relative preference for each good.
- The \(\log\) gives diminishing marginal utility --- eating 10 burritos is not 10 times as good as eating 1.

** Demands Under Cobb-Douglas
Solving the consumer's problem yields a clean result:
\[
  c_i^{*} = \frac{\alpha_i}{\alpha_1 + \alpha_2} \cdot \frac{x}{p_i}
\]

Each good gets a /fixed share/ of the budget.

Example: if \(\alpha_1 = 0.3\) and \(\alpha_2 = 0.7\), then 30% of income goes to good 1 and 70% to good 2, no matter what prices are.

** Is Constant Budget Shares Realistic?
Think about your own spending as income rises:
- Do you spend the same /fraction/ on rice? On restaurant meals?
- Probably not --- this is a limitation of Cobb-Douglas.
- We'll need richer models for the project.

* Demand Curves and Engel Curves
** Demand Curves
Fix income and all other prices.  Vary \(p_1\):

- As \(p_1\) rises, you buy less of good 1 (usually).
- The plot of \(c_1^{*}\) against \(p_1\) is the /demand curve/.
- Convention (blame Alfred & Mary Paley Marshall): price on the vertical axis!

** What Shifts a Demand Curve?
The demand curve for good 1 shifts when:
- /Income/ changes --- you can afford more or less.
- /Other prices/ change --- substitutes become cheaper or more expensive.
- /Preferences/ change --- new information about health, taste, etc.

** Engel Curves
Fix all prices.  Vary the budget \(x\):

- The plot of \(c_i^{*}\) against \(x\) is the /Engel curve/ (named for Ernst Engel, one of your P3 team names!).
Normal goods have demand that rises with income.  /Inferior goods/ have demand that falls with income (e.g., instant noodles).

** Engel's Law
Ernst Engel (1857) observed: as income rises, the /share/ of spending on food falls.

This is one of the most robust empirical findings in economics:
- Poor households spend 50--80% of income on food.
- Rich households may spend 10--15%.
- This has big implications for how price changes affect welfare.

* Beyond Cobb-Douglas
** Why We Need More Flexible Models
Cobb-Douglas forces constant budget shares.  Real data shows:
- Budget shares change with income (Engel's Law).
- Some goods are substitutes; others are complements.
- Responses to price changes differ across foods.

** Constant Frisch Elasticity (CFE) Utility
A more flexible form:
\[
  U(c) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_i \frac{\beta_i}{\beta_i - 1}\left(c_i^{1-1/\beta_i} - 1\right)
\]

- \(\alpha_i\) still governs relative importance of each good.
- \(\beta_i\) controls the /curvature/ --- how quickly marginal utility diminishes.
- When all \(\beta_i = 1\), this reduces to Cobb-Douglas.

** What Changes?
With different \(\beta_i\) values:
- Budget shares now depend on prices and income.
- Engel curves can be nonlinear.
- Demand curves can have different shapes for different goods.

This is closer to the kind of model you'll estimate in your project.
